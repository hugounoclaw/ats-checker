Real Estate Agent ATS Resume Checklist (2026)

12 ATS-resume checks Real Estate Agents need to pass in 2026, the keywords recruiters scan for, and three role-specific resume bullets to copy.

Real estate agent resumes are screened by brokerages, team leads, and recruiters who want to see active license, MLS proficiency, transaction volume, and GCI before anything else. State license number, expiration date, and the MLS systems you've worked in (NTREIS, Stellar, BrightMLS, CRMLS) belong in the header, not buried in Education. Designations — CRS, ABR, GRI, SRES, e-PRO — are the credibility-stackers that separate professional agents from license-only hobbyists.

Production numbers matter more than years licensed. Annual transaction sides, total sales volume, average sale price, list-to-close ratio, and days-on-market versus market average tell a hiring brokerage exactly where you sit. SOI size, CRM (Top Producer, Wise Agent, Follow Up Boss, kvCORE), and lead-source mix (referral, Zillow, Realtor.com, open house) round out a serious agent profile.

The 12-point ATS checklist for Real Estate Agents

  1. Active license number and state in headerList your state, license number, and date issued near your name. Brokerage recruiters verify license status as a knockout first step; making it easy keeps you in the pipeline.
  2. Name the MLS systems you've usedBrightMLS, CRMLS, Stellar, NTREIS, Triangle MLS — list the regional MLS by exact name. Cross-MLS experience matters for agents moving markets.
  3. Quantify annual transaction sidesBuyer sides, seller sides, total sides per year for the last 3 years. Sides are the industry's universal production metric — without them, you have no credibility line.
  4. State total sales volume and GCITotal volume in dollars and gross commission income for each of the last 3 years. Top-tier brokerage recruiters request this in initial screens; including it speeds your funnel.
  5. List NAR designations earnedCRS (Certified Residential Specialist), ABR (Accredited Buyer's Representative), GRI (Graduate REALTOR Institute), SRES (Seniors Specialist), e-PRO. Designations require coursework and signal commitment.
  6. Show CRM and lead-management stackTop Producer, Wise Agent, Follow Up Boss, kvCORE, BoomTown, Chime. Tech-forward brokerages screen for CRM fluency because retention of SOI drives lifetime production.
  7. Include list-to-close ratioListings taken vs listings sold within original list term. Demonstrates pricing accuracy and seller-side competence beyond just buyer-side volume.
  8. Detail days-on-market versus market averageYour average DOM versus your MLS area DOM proves marketing and pricing skill. 'Sold in average 18 DOM versus market 47' is hard evidence of effectiveness.
  9. Quantify SOI and database sizeSphere of Influence count, active database, and annual touches/transactions per 100 SOI. Brokerages buying agents value the book of business that comes with them.
  10. Cover lead-source mixPercentage from SOI/referral, Zillow Premier Agent, Realtor.com, open house, online ads. Diverse lead sources reduce risk; pure portal-lead agents face heavier scrutiny.
  11. List specialty markets and price bandsLuxury ($1M+), first-time buyers, investors, relocation, new construction, REO, short sale. Brokerage team-fit hinges on specialty alignment.
  12. Mention any team lead or mentor roleAgents transitioning to team lead, sales manager, or broker roles need to show leadership — agents mentored, team production, or recruiting wins.

Common ATS rejection reasons for Real Estate Agents

✗ License number and state missing or buried
Fix:Move license info to the header. Brokerage recruiters verify license status as the first step and want it readable in 3 seconds.
✗ No transaction sides or volume listed
Fix:Add a 3-year production table — sides, volume, GCI per year. Without production numbers, brokerages can't slot you into a comp plan or team.
✗ MLS named only as 'MLS' or 'local MLS'
Fix:Name the specific regional MLS. Cross-MLS migration requires re-orientation, so naming yours lets recruiters assess training needed.
✗ No designations beyond license
Fix:Add CRS, ABR, GRI, or specialty designations earned. Even one designation signals continuing-education commitment beyond minimum CE.
✗ Generic 'helped clients buy and sell' bullets
Fix:Replace with specific numbers — sides, volume, DOM, list-to-close. Real estate is a numbers business and recruiters skim for them ruthlessly.

FAQ — Real Estate Agent ATS questions

Do I include GCI/commission earned or just transactions?

Both. Sides and volume show market activity; GCI shows your actual book value to a brokerage. Brokerages buying agents care about GCI because it informs split negotiations and forecasted contribution. If GCI feels too personal, list a range or percentile rank instead.

Should I list the brokerage I'm currently with on a recruiting resume?

Yes — current brokerage is industry-standard and impossible to hide. Frame your reason for exploring with discretion if needed (growth, mentorship, splits, team support). Brokerage recruiters expect agents to evaluate options.

How far back should production numbers go?

Three years standard, five if your trajectory is strong and consistent. If you had a down year due to market or personal reason, include it with brief context — gaps invite worse assumptions than honest numbers do.

Do designations like CRS or ABR actually matter to brokerages?

Yes, especially at brand-name and luxury brokerages where designations are part of agent-page marketing. Even at independent brokerages, designations signal you'll invest in training and reduce ramp-time questions.

Should new agents list pre-real-estate sales or service experience?

Absolutely — first 12-24 months in real estate are a sales job above all else. Prior sales, mortgage, title, construction, or hospitality experience all translate. Frame transferable skills (negotiation, client management, lead conversion) explicitly.
