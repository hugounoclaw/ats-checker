Site Reliability Engineer ATS Resume Checklist (2026)

12 ATS-resume checks Site Reliability Engineers need to pass in 2026, the keywords recruiters scan for, and three role-specific resume bullets to copy.

SRE resumes get screened on a vocabulary set that did not exist 10 years ago: SLOs, SLIs, error budgets, blameless postmortems, p99 latency, and toil reduction. ATS pipelines at companies that actually run SRE (vs DevOps relabeled) look for this exact terminology, and missing it signals you came from a sysadmin or DevOps background without the reliability framing.

In 2026 the role has bifurcated: product SRE (embedded with a service team) and platform SRE (running shared infrastructure). Be explicit about which you are and which the posting wants. Hiring managers also scrutinize on-call claims because inflated rotation numbers are a common red flag.

The 12-point ATS checklist for Site Reliability Engineers

  1. Use SLO/SLI/error-budget vocabulary, not uptimeReplace maintained 99.99 percent uptime with operated a 99.9 percent monthly availability SLO with a 43-minute error budget. The first phrase is marketing, the second tells an SRE you understand the math.
  2. Quote specific p95 or p99 latency targetsHeld p99 checkout latency under 220 ms across 14 release cycles is uniquely SRE. p95/p99 numbers are rare on non-SRE resumes and immediately mark you as the right candidate.
  3. Name your on-call rotation honestlyPrimary on-call 1 in 6, 7-day rotation, ~3 paged incidents per shift. Hiring managers spot inflated 1 in 4 with no acks claims fast. Honest scope reads senior.
  4. Mention error-budget policy enforcement, not just measurementTriggered a feature-freeze when checkout SLO breached for 2 consecutive weeks shows you operate the policy, not just set the SLO and forget.
  5. List your alerting tool with a noise-reduction winCut Alertmanager noise from 320 to 41 weekly pages by deleting symptom alerts and adding multi-window burn-rate alerts. Noise reduction is a top SRE deliverable.
  6. Show postmortem authorship countAuthored 11 blameless postmortems and led 6 incident reviews proves cultural fit at SRE-mature orgs. Mention the template (Google SRE, Etsy, internal) if you adapted one.
  7. Name your observability stack across all three pillarsPrometheus + Grafana + Loki + Tempo, or Datadog APM + Logs + RUM, or Honeycomb + OTel Collector. SRE screens distinguish candidates by which three-pillar stack they actually operate.
  8. Show capacity-planning or load-testing workRan quarterly load tests with k6 hitting 4x peak, identified Postgres connection-pool ceiling at 2.1k req/s. Capacity work is rare on resumes and high-signal.
  9. Mention chaos or failure-injection if realLitmus, Chaos Mesh, Gremlin, or homegrown fault injection. Only claim this if you actually ran game days; SRE interviewers probe deeply because it is a common overclaim.
  10. Quote toil-reduction or automation hours savedAutomated certificate rotation across 84 services, eliminating ~12 engineering hours per quarter. Toil reduction is core SRE doctrine and the bullet writes itself if you measured it.
  11. Distinguish service ownership from oncall coverageOn-call for 4 owned services, secondary for 12 shared platform services. SRE hiring managers want to know the ownership boundary because it predicts onboarding speed.
  12. Show a kernel, network, or DB deep-dive bulletTracked p99 spike to TCP retransmits caused by SNAT port exhaustion on the NAT gateway. One systems-deep bullet separates SRE from DevOps in the eyes of the reviewer.

Common ATS rejection reasons for Site Reliability Engineers

✗ Claims SRE experience but uses 99.99 percent uptime instead of SLOs
Fix:Rewrite at least three bullets using SLO, SLI, and error-budget terminology with actual percentages and budget minutes.
✗ On-call rotation listed as 1 in 3 or 1 in 4 for years on end
Fix:Either reflect the real rotation honestly or explain a temporary burst (covered 1 in 3 during 6-month team transition); reviewers spot fake numbers in interviews.
✗ Generic observability bullet with no specific tool
Fix:Name the metrics, logs, and traces backend you used; vague observability means nothing to SRE hiring managers.
✗ Postmortem mentioned but no template, count, or follow-up actions
Fix:Add the count, the template you followed, and at least one action item that shipped (e.g. removed a class of incident permanently).

FAQ — Site Reliability Engineer ATS questions

How much on-call do I need to list to look senior?

A senior SRE typically shows 12-24 months of primary on-call on a 1-in-5 or 1-in-6 rotation plus incident command experience. More than that risks reading as burnt-out coverage rather than ownership. Quality of post-incident artifacts matters more than raw hours.

Does mentioning a 4-nines SLO get flagged as fake?

Often yes, especially for product services. 99.99 percent leaves only 4.3 minutes of monthly downtime which is unachievable for most stacks. Use the SLO that was actually agreed with the product team; 99.9 percent or 99.95 percent reads more credible and is what most real services run.

Should I list specific incident severities I handled?

Yes if your org used a clear scale (SEV1 through SEV4). Phrase it as led 6 SEV1 and 14 SEV2 incidents in 2025. Concrete severity counts are highly credible and rare on non-SRE resumes.

Is Kubernetes mandatory for SRE roles in 2026?

For about 80 percent of SRE postings, yes. The remaining 20 percent are at orgs running large bare-metal or VM fleets (gaming, HFT, telco). If you have no K8s, target those explicitly rather than padding the resume.

Do I list the SRE book or Google course on my resume?

No. Reading the SRE book is assumed at this point and listing it reads as junior. Internalize the vocabulary and let your bullets demonstrate it, which is far more convincing.
