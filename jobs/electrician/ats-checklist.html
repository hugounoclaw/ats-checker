Electrician ATS Resume Checklist (2026)

12 ATS-resume checks Electricians need to pass in 2026, the keywords recruiters scan for, and three role-specific resume bullets to copy.

Electrical contractors (Rosendin, Cupertino Electric, Faith Technologies), industrial maintenance shops, and facilities teams process electrician applications through Workday, iCIMS, hiring-manager direct channels, and union dispatch (IBEW Local halls). Almost every commercial and industrial req filters on state journeyman or master license and current NEC code-cycle familiarity. Apprentices are filtered on hours logged toward state journeyman exam eligibility.

The next triage layer is scope — residential, commercial, or industrial — plus conduit and motor-control specifics. "Electrician" is generic; "journeyman electrician with motor-control / VFD / PLC start-up experience on 480V three-phase" reads to an industrial recruiter as immediately deployable. IBEW-vs-non-union, OSHA 30, and bend-and-pull conduit competency round out the screen.

The 12-point ATS checklist for Electricians

  1. State license: journeyman or master with numberWrite "Journeyman Electrician, State of Texas, License #JE-12345, exp 06/2027" or "Master Electrician." Some states issue residential-specific or industrial-specific licenses — list the exact class. Compliance gates filter on it.
  2. Apprentice hours toward license, if applicableList hours logged (e.g., "6,400 of 8,000 hours toward TX journeyman exam eligibility") and OJT classroom completion. Apprentice reqs filter on hour count and program (IBEW NJATC/NEIEP, IEC, ABC).
  3. NEC code cycle currencyState "NEC 2023 trained" (or current adopted cycle in your state). Many states adopt cycles late — note both the latest you've studied and your state's adopted cycle. Recruiters filter on "NEC compliant" plus a year.
  4. Scope clearly labeled per roleResidential (single-family, multi-family), commercial (TI, ground-up), industrial (process plant, manufacturing), utility, data center, healthcare. Reqs are scope-specific and the wrong label routes you to the wrong queue.
  5. Voltage range and three-phase experienceNote 120/208/240/277/480V, three-phase, and any medium-voltage (>1000V) work. Industrial and data center reqs filter for three-phase 480V and MV experience explicitly.
  6. Conduit bending and rough-in volumeEMT, IMC, rigid GRC, PVC, MC cable, aluminum vs copper. Mention hand bender, mechanical bender, hydraulic Chicago bender, segment bending of large conduit. Industrial GCs filter for this.
  7. Motor control, VFDs, and PLC start-upMotor starters, soft starts, VFDs (Allen-Bradley PowerFlex, ABB, Yaskawa, Eaton), PLC I/O termination and start-up (AB ControlLogix, Siemens S7, Modicon). Industrial reqs filter on motor control and PLC.
  8. Low-voltage / structured cabling separateCat6/Cat6A, fiber (single-mode, multi-mode, fusion splicing), BICSI certification if held, access control, fire alarm (NICET I-IV). Many commercial roles want low-voltage cross-trained electricians.
  9. OSHA 10/30 and NFPA 70E arc-flash trainingOSHA 10 or 30 Construction, NFPA 70E (annual) for arc-flash, LOTO, confined space, fall protection, scissor/boom lift. Commercial and industrial sites won't badge you without 70E.
  10. IBEW Local and book status, if applicableList "IBEW Local 134, Book 1" if union; or note "non-union, open shop." Union signatory contractors hire through hall dispatch and want to know your local and book status.
  11. Tools owned and specialty equipmentStandard belt + specialty: Megger insulation tester, Fluke 1587 / 87V, Ideal SureTest, thermal imaging camera, hydraulic crimpers, Chicago bender, hi-pot for MV. Tool ownership signals deployment readiness.
  12. Estimating, layout, and BIM/Revit familiarityIf foreman track: blueprint reading, NEC code-of-record interpretation, prefab coordination, Revit MEP / Bluebeam, time-and-material billing. GC-focused reqs and foreman ladders filter on these.

Common ATS rejection reasons for Electricians

✗ License or apprentice hours missing
Fix:Add Licensure section with license class, state, number, and expiration — or apprentice hours logged with program name (IBEW NJATC/NEIEP, IEC, ABC).
✗ NEC code cycle not referenced
Fix:State NEC 2023 (or current adopted cycle) trained; reqs filter on code-cycle currency, not just "NEC compliant."
✗ Scope mismatch (residential resume on commercial req)
Fix:Rewrite emphasizing relevant voltage, conduit type, and scope. A 120/240V residential-only profile won't score on a 480V three-phase industrial req.
✗ No motor control / PLC for industrial role
Fix:Add motor starter, VFD, and PLC start-up experience with named OEM (Allen-Bradley, Siemens) — industrial GCs filter on these.
✗ NFPA 70E absent for commercial/industrial
Fix:Add NFPA 70E arc-flash annual training; large GC sites won't badge you without it.
✗ IBEW status unclear
Fix:Specify local and book if union, or "non-union/open shop" — signatory contractors hire through hall dispatch and want clarity upfront.

FAQ — Electrician ATS questions

Do I list NEC code year or just say 'NEC compliant'?

List the year. NEC adopts new cycles every three years and states adopt at different speeds — "NEC 2023 trained" tells a recruiter you've sat through the latest update. "NEC compliant" alone reads as filler. If your state is still on NEC 2020, note both: "NEC 2023 trained; TX adopted NEC 2020 currently."

Should I include apprentice hours if I'm not licensed yet?

Yes — apprentice reqs filter directly on hour count and program. "6,400 of 8,000 hours toward journeyman exam, IBEW NJATC/NEIEP Year 4" lets a contractor place you accurately on the wage scale. Without hours, you read as a year-one helper and get screened out of intermediate work.

Does IBEW vs non-union matter on the resume?

It matters for the contractor you're applying to. Union signatory GCs hire through Local halls (Book 1/2 dispatch); listing your local and book signals you're dispatch-eligible. Non-union shops sometimes pass on union-card holders for retention reasons. Be explicit so neither side wastes time.

How do I show PLC / motor control experience without claiming I'm a controls engineer?

Be specific about what you did: "terminated I/O," "started up VFDs," "assisted controls engineer with point-to-point checkout," "replaced VFD and re-parameterized." That language matches industrial-electrician reqs without overstating into a controls-tech role.

Is OSHA 30 really expected or is OSHA 10 enough?

OSHA 10 is the minimum for most commercial sites; OSHA 30 is increasingly required for foreman track and large GC sites, and many states (NY, MO) mandate OSHA 10 minimum on public works. NFPA 70E is the arc-flash credential — annual refresh is standard. Add both if you have them.
